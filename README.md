# Telecom-Churn-Analysis
The analysis shows a 15% of the customers churned. Non-churners are more engaged through frequent service calls, while churners show disengagement despite higher peak-hour usage. Account tenure doesn’t impact churn. Churn is highest in New Jersey and lowest in Iowa.
1.High call frequency among non-churners indicates strong engagement.
Customers who remain with the company actively contact customer service, suggesting they still perceive value in the service and trust that their issues will be resolved.
2.Lower call frequency among churners suggests disengagement.
Customers who churn tend to make fewer service calls, indicating they may have already disengaged and are no longer attempting to resolve issues—possibly waiting for contract completion or better competitor offers.
3. Call intent of loyal customers requires deeper analysis.
Further investigation is needed to understand whether calls from loyal customers are related to plan upgrades, technical issues, billing concerns, or other service requests.
4.Higher usage during peak hours is observed among churners.
Churners show higher average day and evening minutes, which may indicate:
a) Technical or network issues during peak usage hours, or
b) Migration to competitors offering better unlimited plans, rewards, or high-usage benefits.
5.Account tenure does not differentiate churn behavior.
The average account length is similar for both churners and non-churners, suggesting that customer longevity alone does not guarantee retention.
6.Overall churn rate stands at 15%.
Non-churners account for 85% of the customer base. The highest churn is observed in New Jersey, while the lowest churn is recorded in Iowa.
